Level 7 of 7

Design extraction

The strongest lever: extract the full design language of a site you love, and rebuild your product in that language. Translation, not cloning.

The technique

Most people can't articulate why a design is great — 'ample white space' and 'golden ratio' only go so far. A design-extraction skill does it for you: it pulls tokens, typography, easing curves, and load-bearing CSS rules from a target site into a builder brief.

This round both runs pointed the skill at teenage.engineering (round 1 used antigravity.google) — chosen because it serves a single public root stylesheet with 98 :root custom properties, exactly what the skill's CSS sweep assumes, and its industrial-hardware minimalism rhymes with a Cybertruck. The skill lives in this repo under skill/design-language/, byte-identical to round 1's copy.

The prompts that did it

Extract, then rebuild in that language
Hi there. I'm going to give you a website. I would like to basically understand the design, and I'd like you to follow all the instructions in this file, Namely go to this website, understand the typography, the design, and get it an extraction blueprint so that I can build a website that levels this up. And our website is going to be on selling beautiful trucks. @design-language-skill.zip https://teenage.engineering/

Both models delivered an extraction kit first — brief, tokens.json, structure.css, scaffold, preview — not a finished page. Fable wrote its kit to ~/Desktop instead of the working directory (agents have habits too); Opus 4.8 kept its kit in the trial dir and asked how to fill the {{BRAND}} placeholders.

Push from blueprint to deliverable (follow-up, both models)
I didn't find the website file, where is it? The scaffold only has the framework, not the real website.

Both promoted the kit to the deliverable — and both landed on ~22 KB, lowercase, teenage.engineering-voice pages. Fable stayed unbranded: "the beautiful truck." Opus 4.8 reused its level-3 brand: "CYBR — Built for Mars. Painted for Miami." A second scripted follow-up (rename the file) never fired; both files were already correctly named.

Cost & time

What changed vs level 6

  • Night and day versus level 1 — a coherent, opinionated design identity with real restraint.
  • The convergence beat repeats: round 1 gave Lume/LUMA, round 2 gives twin ~22 KB lowercase pages in the same voice.
  • The output is a reusable design system, not just one page.

What this level taught

  • Design extraction gives Claude the vocabulary of a specific great design — the thing humans struggle to articulate.
  • A blueprint is an intermediate artifact; budget one more prompt to promote it to the deliverable (true in both rounds).
  • Name the destination directory too — Fable's kit went to ~/Desktop out of habit.
  • Convergence is a property of the technique, not the model: same language in, same voice out.
